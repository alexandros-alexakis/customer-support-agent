# Common Failure Patterns

## Overview

This document tracks the most frequent quality failures observed in support interactions. It is updated monthly based on QA review data and used to inform training priorities.

---

## Failure Pattern 1: Skipping Empathy

**What it looks like:**
Agent goes straight to troubleshooting or information collection without acknowledging the player's frustration.

**Example:**
> Player: "I've been waiting 3 days and nobody has helped me, this is ridiculous."
> Agent: "Please provide your Player ID and transaction details."

**Why it happens:**
Agents focus on efficiency and resolving the issue quickly, treating empathy as optional.

**Impact:**
Player feels dismissed. CSAT drops significantly even when the issue is resolved.

**Correction:**
Always acknowledge before moving to resolution. One sentence is enough: "I completely understand your frustration, and I'm sorry for the wait. Let me look into this for you right now."

---

## Failure Pattern 2: Unauthorized Promises

**What it looks like:**
Agent implies or directly states that a refund, compensation, or specific outcome will happen.

**Example:**
> "Don't worry, we'll get those items restored for you."
> "I'm sure our team will approve your refund."

**Why it happens:**
Agents want to reassure players and reduce friction in the conversation.

**Impact:**
If the promise isn't delivered, player anger escalates significantly. Creates liability.

**Correction:**
Only commit to what you can guarantee: the escalation, the timeframe for review, and that the case will be looked at. Never the outcome.

---

## Failure Pattern 3: Escalating Without Sufficient Information

**What it looks like:**
Agent escalates a ticket without collecting the Player ID, transaction ID, device info, or other required details.

**Why it happens:**
Agent wants to escalate quickly to avoid dealing with a difficult case.

**Impact:**
Specialist team cannot action the ticket. It gets bounced back, causing delay and player frustration.

**Correction:**
Refer to the escalation checklist before escalating. Every escalation must include minimum required information.

---

## Failure Pattern 4: Incorrect Scope Handling

**What it looks like:**
Agent attempts to resolve an out-of-scope issue rather than escalating, or escalates an issue that should have been resolved at Tier 1.

**Why it happens:**
Agent is unclear on the scope boundaries or lacks confidence in their own ability to resolve.

**Correction:**
Review the escalation rules document. When unsure, ask a senior agent or team lead before escalating.

---

## Failure Pattern 5: Closing Without Confirming Resolution

**What it looks like:**
Agent closes the ticket after providing a response without confirming the player's issue was actually resolved.

**Why it happens:**
Pressure to keep handling time low leads to premature ticket closure.

**Impact:**
Player has to recontact, which increases ticket volume and damages FCR metrics.

**Correction:**
Always end with: "Is there anything else I can help you with?" or "Does this fully resolve your issue?" before closing.
